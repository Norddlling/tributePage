Sir Alexander Fleming

The man who discovered penicillin and saved millions lives

This summarizes Fleming's biography:

  • 6 August 1881 - Born on at Lochfield farm near Darvel, in Ayrshire, Scotland
  • 1903 - Alexander enrolled at St Mary's Hospital Medical School in Paddington. He qualified with an MBBS degree from the school with distinction in 1906.
  • 1900 - 1914 Fleming was a private in the London Scottish Regiment of the Volunteer Force
  • 1914 - He was commissioned lieutenant and promoted captain in 1917, served throughout World War I in the Royal Army Medical Corps, and was Mentioned in Dispatches. He worked in battlefield hospitals at the Western Front in France
  • 24 December 1915 - Fleming married a trained nurse, Sarah Marion McElroy of Killala, County Mayo, Ireland.
  • 1917 - Fleming published article in the medical journal The Lancet where he described an ingenious experiment, which he was able to conduct as a result of his own glass blowing skills, in which he explained why antiseptics were killing more soldiers than infection itself during the war.
  • 1921 - While he was maintaining agar plates for bacteria, he found that one of the plates was contaminated with bacteria from the air. When he added nasal mucus, he found that the mucus inhibited the bacterial growth. Thats how he discover of lysozyme - an antimicrobial enzyme produced by animals that forms part of the innate immune system.
  • 1924 - Fleming had a son Robert Fleming
  • 3 September 1928 - Fleming returned to his laboratory having spent a holiday with his family at Suffolk. He inoculated staphylococci on culture plates and left them on a bench in a corner of his laboratory. Fleming noticed that one culture was contaminated with a fungus, and that the colonies of staphylococci immediately surrounding the fungus had been destroyed, whereas other staphylococci colonies farther away were normal. That how was discovered of penicillin
  • 1932 - Fleming also successfully treated severe conjunctivitis. That was the first case to be treated clinically with penicillin ointment
  • 1940 - Ernst Boris Chain and Edward Abraham published its first results in studying the molecular structure of the antibiotic
  • 1942 - The Oxford team produced the pure penicillin compound as yellow powder.
  • 1943 - Fleming was elected a Fellow of the Royal Society (FRS)
  • 1944 - penicillin had been produced to treat all the wounded of the Allied troops by D-Day
  • 1944 - Fleming was knighted, as a Knight Bachelor, by king George VI
  • 1945 - Fleming, Florey and Chain jointly received the Nobel Prize in Medicine
  • 1948 - He was made a Knight Grand Cross of the Order of Alfonso X the Wise (Spain)
  • 1949 - After his first wife's death Fleming married Amalia Koutsouri-Vourekas, a Greek colleague at St. Mary's
  • 11 March 1955 - Fleming died at his home in London of a heart attack. His ashes are buried in St Paul's Cathedral.

In 1999, Time magazine named Fleming one of the 100 Most Important People of the 20th century, stating:

It was a discovery that would change the course of history. The active ingredient in that mould, which Fleming named penicillin, turned out to be an infection-fighting agent of enormous potency. When it was finally recognized for what it was, the most efficacious life-saving drug in the world, penicillin would alter forever the treatment of bacterial infections. By the middle of the century, Fleming's discovery had spawned a huge pharmaceutical industry, churning out synthetic penicillins that would conquer some of mankind's most ancient scourges, including syphilis, gangrene and tuberculosis.
